Starter Guide

Why Credit Cards?

It's not just about cashback. Take a look at some lesser known perks below!

Free Travel

Earn points and miles that you can redeem for flights and hotels

Benefits & Status

Enjoy perks such as lounge access, room upgrades, free checked bags, and priority boarding

Expedited Screening

Skip long security and immigration lines with TSA PreCheck and Global Entry, available for free with many credit cards

Fraud Protection

Unlike cash or debit cards, banks are responsible for unauthorized transactions on your credit card, so you will never have to worry about losing money again.

Insurance

Protect yourself from the unexpected with benefits like Trip Delay and Lost Baggage Insurance, Rental Insurance, and Purchase Protection

Build Credit

Contrary to popular belief, having more cards help improve your credit score in the long term

Personal Experiences

Times when credit cards have made my life better

EVA Air Business Class

Last summer, I got to experience EVA Air's Royal Laurel business class product, regarded as one of the best in the industry. The entire trip would've cost me over $8000+, but with points and miles, I only had to pay $52 in taxes.

Expedited Immigration

I use to find myself waiting upwards of an hour in line to clear US customs & immigration after a 12-hour international flight. With Global Entry, a free perk that came with my Chase Sapphire Reserve, I was able to clear immigration in just 30 seconds.

Expedited Security

I usually carry two laptops, an iPad, a DLSR, 3 lenses, etc when traveling. Having TSA Precheck, which came with my Global Entry membership, allows me to skip the long wait times and avoid removing my electronics, shoes, and belts when passing through security.

Purchase Protection & Extended Warranty

When I lost my AirPods on a bus, I utilized "Purchase Protection" and Chase was able to reimburse me for my replacement. When my MacBook Pro broke down, Chase was again able to reimburse me for a replacement purchase under the "Extended Warranty" perk.

Lounge Access

Even when flying economy, the priority pass benefit allows me to access more than 1300 lounges worldwide. Examples include access to Air China first class lounge at PEK where there is a noodle bar that serves 炸醬麵 on request, a delicious Taiwanese 牛肉麵 at Plaza Premium TPE, or dining at a restaurant with a free $28 dining credit at IAD during my layover. Free Stay at Marriott

As part of my 100,000 pt sign up bonus I earned from the Amex Business Marriott card, I redeemed 17,500pts (cash value ~$170) for a night at the Courtyard Downtown Taipei with my parents. Even with only silver status that came with the card, I was able to score a free Taipei-101 facing room upgrade!

Let's get started!

Follow these simple steps to make sure you have the basics

1. Get a SSN

Although there are ways around this, the simplest way is to get a Social Security Number to build history. If you are not a US citizen, you can apply for a SSN once you have a job offer.

2. Check your score

The easiest way is to sign up for an account with Credit Karma (which is what I use). Many banks and services (Mint, NerdWallet, etc) will also offer free credit reports, although I found the UI and explanation of your score best using CK. This will help you gauge which cards you may be eligible for and potential factors impacting your score.

3. Determine your goals

What is your main objective for getting credit cards? There is no single best card, and what is good depends on every individual's unique situation. Figuring out what your needs are will help you determine the best cards for YOU. Below are some common reasons:

  • Points or Cashback
  • Airline Status & Perks
  • Hotel Status & Perks
  • Preferred Boarding
  • Free Checked Bags
  • Expedited Security and Immigration (Global Entry & TSA Pre)
  • Lounge Access
  • Maximize everyday spend
  • Free Business/First Class Flights
  • Rental Car Status, Perks & Insurance
  • No foreign transaction fees
  • Purchase Protection or Return Protection

General Strategy

While with credit cards, the optimal strategy can vary for each individual based on their unique situation. This is the general strategy I follow:

1. Get a starter card

Getting approved for your first credit card is often the hardest step. Don't expect great rewards for this first card, just be patient as this card will help build your credit history. It is generally recommended to wait 3-6 months before applying for your next card.

2. Get Chase cards

Chase has a rule called the 5/24 rule. Essentially you will not be approved for any Chase cards if you have 5 or more credit cards from any banks within the last 24 months. While this may sound like a lot, your cards may build up fast once you start, so it is always good to get Chase cards first as you can always come back to the other banks later.

3. Get cards with high signup bonuses

While you may earn 3-5x on “dining”, the earning potential for signup bonuses can be in the 15-30x range. As such, you should look for cards with high sign up bonuses (for example, Chase Sapphire Preferred, spend $4000 in 3 months, get 60,000 UR points, equivalent to $600 in cash or up to $1200 in travel!).

4. Advanced Techniques

There are so many ways to push your earning potential even further. Tactics such as getting business cards, paying large payments like rent and mortgage using a service called Plastiq, and double/triple-dipping using reward portals like Rakuten.

Important Note: While this represents a general guide, it is the sole opinion of myself and based on what has worked for me. Most card recommendations are cards I have and like on my spending and traveling pattern. The tips mentioned are also mostly based on what I have learned from other resources and proven useful for myself. It is important to know that regarding credit cards, rules are constantly changing, and what has worked for one person before may not work as well or no longer work for others. I highly recommend reading through each card's full details or reach out to me for questions before pulling the trigger.
